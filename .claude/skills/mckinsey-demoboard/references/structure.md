# Demoboard structure — page-by-page scaffold

A demoboard has a fixed page architecture. Follow it. Improvisation weakens the format; consistency strengthens it.

## The eight-page template

```
1.  Cover                     — assertion headline + lede + metadata strip
2.  Executive summary         — 4 numbered takeaways (one per major section)
3.  The problem               — diagnostic exhibit (what's wrong today)
4.  The frame                 — structural exhibit (three gaps, two axes, etc.)
5.  Market positioning        — where you sit vs. alternatives
--- HINGE PAGE (dark) ---     — one-sentence thesis, full-bleed
6.  Product / solution        — architecture exhibit + MECE detail
7.  The key artifact          — the single thing that proves the argument
8.  Engagement / rollout      — timeline or maturity curve
--- AGENDA PAGE ---           — 6 discussion items + sign-off
```

Total substantive pages: 4–7 (cover and exec summary don't count toward the exhibit budget; neither does the agenda).

## Page 1 — Cover

The cover must contain exactly these elements, in this order:

- **Mono eyebrow** (small uppercase, colored with the accent): "Pre-read · Prepared exclusively for [Recipient]"
- **Main headline**: an assertion, not a label. Italicize or accent-color the key phrase. 40–72px serif, light weight.
- **Lede paragraph**: 2–4 sentences. Names the recipient's situation and positions the document as a tool for a specific decision or conversation. Serif, 17–20px, light weight.
- **Metadata strip**: 4 cells, each with a mono label (Document / Audience / Read time / Purpose) and a short value. Bordered top and bottom with a solid rule.

Do NOT put a product screenshot, logo lockup, or tagline on the cover. The cover must read as a document cover, not a marketing landing page.

## Page 2 — Executive summary

This is the pyramid-principle page. If the reader stops here, they still have the full argument.

- Page header with section number `01` and label "Executive summary"
- Assertion headline that states the meta-argument (e.g., "Four takeaways should guide our conversation — [Product] is a category, not a tool.")
- Subhead: "Each of the four summarizes a section of this pre-read. If only one page is read, this is the one."
- 2×2 grid of four takeaways, each with:
  - Roman numeral (I, II, III, IV) in mono accent color
  - Assertion-style sub-headline (one sentence)
  - Two-sentence explanation

Each takeaway must map to a later section. If you have five sections, pick the four most important.

## Pages 3–5 — The setup (problem, frame, positioning)

These three pages establish the world before introducing the product. McKinsey structure — never lead with the product.

- **The problem page**: a diagnostic exhibit showing what's wrong with the status quo. Value chain, process flow, or "today vs. should-be" comparison. Assertion headline names the root cause.
- **The frame page**: a structural exhibit making a MECE decomposition of the problem. Three gaps, two axes, four quadrants. This is the analytical frame the rest of the document will reference.
- **The positioning page**: a 2×2 matrix, a landscape comparison, or a "vs. alternatives" decomposition. Shows where you sit relative to things the reader is already considering.

## The hinge page

A single dark full-bleed page between the setup and the product. One sentence of serif italic type on a dark background, positioning the thesis.

This page is deliberately thin on content. It's a pause — a moment of visual rhythm that marks the transition from "here's the problem" to "here's what we do about it." Do not skip it. Do not add more content to it.

The sentence should be the most quotable thing in the document. If someone forwarded this demoboard and quoted one sentence, this is it.

## Pages 6–7 — The solution (product, key artifact)

- **The product page**: an architecture exhibit (module diagram, value chain with your product in it, or system schematic) plus a MECE decomposition of the components. Do NOT use a feature list. Use a structural view.
- **The key artifact page**: the single most important thing the product produces. A trust score, a decision log, a certified data product, a P&L forecast — whatever the product's singular output is. Show it with a data visualization (waterfall, gauge, scorecard) — this is the proof page.

## Page 8 — Engagement / rollout

- A timeline, maturity curve, or phased plan showing what the first 30-90-180 days look like. Anchor it on a specific, dated outcome. "By day 90, [specific deliverable]."
- This page is where the document converts from analysis to action.

## The agenda page (final)

- Page header with section number and "Working session agenda"
- Assertion headline: "The goal of our meeting is a working session, not a demo." (Or a variant — the pattern is: reframe the meeting.)
- Subhead explaining the intent
- 2×3 grid of agenda items (6 total). Each item has:
  - Two-digit number in mono accent
  - 4-6 word sub-headline (assertion style)
  - One sentence of explanation
- Sign-off block at the bottom with author names (serif), role (sans, small), and a mono "Confidential · [document version] · [date]" tag on the right

The last agenda item should always be "Open questions" — framed as inviting the reader's critical thinking, not closing the meeting.

## What to never include

- A "thank you" slide
- A "next steps" slide (the agenda IS the next steps)
- A "contact us" slide with phone numbers
- Logos of past customers or endorsements
- Awards, Gartner quadrants, or analyst citations
- A feature comparison table (use a 2×2 or structural exhibit instead)
- Stock photography of people in offices
- Any illustration that isn't an exhibit carrying analytical weight

A McKinsey demoboard earns attention through the density and rigor of its argument. Everything else is noise.
