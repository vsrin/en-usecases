Pre-read · AI-Ready Claims Intelligence

Claims intelligence fails not because AI models are weak, but because the data they consume was never designed for machines.

The claims lifecycle spans ten interconnected stages, each producing structured records and unstructured documents that must converge at every decision gate. Today, that convergence does not happen. This document presents how an ontology-grounded semantic layer, governed knowledge retrieval, and a deterministic-first processing architecture close the gap from raw claims data to AI-ready intelligence.

01 Executive summary One page · Four takeaways

Four structural observations frame why claims AI consistently underdelivers, and how ontology-grounded intelligence changes the equation.

Each of the four summarizes a section of this pre-read. If only one page is read, this is the one.

I.

Claims data spans ten stages and two modalities, but no shared vocabulary connects them.

Every claim produces both structured system records and unstructured documents. Without a shared ontology, every AI initiative becomes a custom integration project that re-solves the same mapping problem.

II.

FNOL is the most consequential and least structured decision gate in the lifecycle.

First Notice of Loss sets coverage expectations, reserve estimates, and fraud posture. It is inherently mixed: a structured policy lookup combined with a free-text loss description. The ontology must bridge both worlds simultaneously.

III.

An ontology-grounded semantic layer converts raw claims data into AI-ready data products with shared entity references.

The ontology defines entities, attributes, and relationships. The semantic layer maps those definitions to physical data products. Together they produce a vocabulary that AI systems, analysts, and adjusters all share.

IV.

ElevateNow enCodex delivers a governance-first claims intelligence platform with an 8:2 deterministic-to-LLM ratio.

A seven-stage processing architecture ensures that regex, formula, and rules-based tools run before any LLM call. Every decision gate is auditable, every state change is versioned, and every payment amount is computed deterministically.

05 The solution architecture Exhibit 4 of 7

Three layers share one ontology and produce one outcome: AI-ready claims data products with full provenance.

The architecture diagram shows the flow from source data through the ontology semantic layer and governed knowledge platform to AI-ready data products. The three MECE columns below decompose what each layer does.

Exhibit 4 · enCodex claims intelligence architecture: ontology, knowledge, governance
Layer 01 · Ontology
Semantic Layer
Defines the nouns, verbs, and rules that make claims data machine-readable.
What it does
  • Defines entity classes: ClaimRecord, LossEvent, CoverageUnit, PolicyRecord, DocumentArtifact
  • Maps relationships: Claim has Line, Line involves Party, Party handled by Adjuster
  • Decomposes losses into adjudicable units via ClaimLine and ClaimItem entities
  • Anchors every claim entity to a PolicyRecord and one or more CoverageUnit instances
  • Bridges structured and unstructured data through shared entity references at every decision gate
Claims application
At FNOL, the ontology simultaneously extracts structured signals from narrative (LossType, LossLocation, InjuryDescription) and preserves the original text as a DocumentArtifact for audit and AI training.
Layer 02 · Knowledge
Governed Knowledge Retrieval
Curates, versions, and retrieves domain knowledge deterministically at runtime.
What it does
  • Stores jurisdiction-specific rules as versioned knowledge chunks (50-state coverage)
  • Maintains indicator trigger catalogs with trigger_field and trigger_condition definitions
  • Provides deterministic chunk lookup (not vector search) for rule application at every gate
  • Supports SME authoring and version control through CurationStudio interface
  • Feeds coverage rules, exclusion logic, and benefit calculations at runtime
Claims application
When a WC claim arrives from New York, the knowledge platform retrieves New York-specific benefit rates, waiting periods, compliance deadlines, and caps. No LLM is involved in the rule lookup.
Layer 03 · Governance
Audit Trail & Authority
Ensures every decision is traceable, every state change is versioned, and every dollar is deterministic.
What it does
  • Forward-only audit trail: no claim record is silently overwritten
  • Ephemeral-to-committed pattern: no mid-level result persists until adjuster confirms
  • Five-dimension authority gate: compensability, indicators, reserve, defenses, subrogation
  • Three-tier indicator contract: Confirmed (penalizes gate), Detected (visible), Rejected (on record)
  • Every tool execution, knowledge chunk cited, and reassessment is logged with timestamps
Claims application
At adjudication, the governance layer captures the decision rationale as a versioned record referencing which documents were reviewed, which coverage sections applied, and what exceptions were granted. Every payment traces back to the document that justified it.
Read together. The ontology gives the vocabulary. The knowledge platform gives the rules. The governance layer gives the audit trail. None works without the other two, and all three are required before any claims data product can be called AI-ready.
06 Technical pipeline Exhibit 5 of 7

A seven-stage deterministic-first pipeline processes every FNOL with an 8:2 deterministic-to-LLM ratio and a maximum of three LLM calls per claim.

The exhibit maps the full processing architecture from PDF upload through intelligence report assembly, showing which stages use LLM and which are purely deterministic.

Exhibit 5 · enCodex seven-stage FNOL processing pipeline with LLM call budget
Why this matters. The 8:2 deterministic-to-LLM ratio is not a design preference. It is a governance requirement. Every dollar amount, every jurisdiction rule, every authority threshold is computed by formula or database lookup. LLM calls are confined to two tasks: extracting structured data from unstructured documents (Stage 0) and confirming indicator detections (Stage 3). The result is a pipeline where every output is auditable and every reserve is reproducible.

All writes flow through recipes. The backend is read-only. This is a governance-first architecture, not a speed-first one.
